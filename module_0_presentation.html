The models most of us start with

Reasonable assumptions —
and where they carry you

"It runs top to bottom, like a script"

Natural — that's how code works, and how tutorials read networks. It predicts behavior correctly in small patches, which is why it survives so long.

carries you → small patches

"Every node executes 60× a second"

Follows from the first one. And it feels true — moving things really do update every frame.

carries you → anything animated

"More nodes = slower project"

A fair inference from the above. It leads people to spend real optimization effort deleting and consolidating nodes — effort pointed at the wrong place.

carries you → tidy networks, same frame drops

Nobody here is wrong. These models work — up to a point. Let's find the point.

The actual model
A dish gets cooked when it's ordered
AND an ingredient changed.

Both conditions. A dish sitting ready under the heat lamp gets served instantly — no re-cook, no cost. A menu with 500 dishes isn't a slow kitchen. A kitchen re-cooking the same dish every minute when nothing changed — that's a slow kitchen.

The core of the whole course
Being on the menu
≠ being cooked.
The cost is the cook.

500 dishes under heat lamps

A huge static network. Costs almost nothing. Don't fear node count.

1 dish re-cooked pointlessly, 60× a second

One small node churning every frame. Costs more than all 500 combined.

Time-dependence

The soufflé problem

Some dishes can't sit under a heat lamp. They must be made fresh — to order, every order: anything containing "now". Time itself. A live camera. A microphone.

One made-to-order ingredient makes every dish downstream of it made-to-order too. Forever.

That's not bad — it's the price of motion. The sin is paying soufflé prices on a dish nothing about needed to be fresh.
The sentence to carry into every module
Don't overcook.
Make the PC's life easier.

Every module after this one answers the same question: where should this computation live, and what is it costing you? Cost means cooking.

Families as data types

Choosing a family is
choosing which kitchen cooks

The natural question

"What do I want to do?" → grab whichever node does it. Works fine in small patches.

The more powerful question

"What is the shape of my data?" Channels of numbers over time. Grids of pixels. Points in space. Tables and text. The family decides where — and how expensively — your data cooks. You're choosing a cost model before you've built anything.

Two kitchens, one restaurant

Fine dining vs the staple kitchen

CPU · CHOPs · DATs · Python · SOPs

Fine dining

  • One master chef. Can cook anything — intricate, exquisite, fully custom plates. Logic, branching, one-of-a-kind dishes.
  • But: one chef, one dish at a time. TD gives you one chef, not a brigade — the single-threaded reality.
  • Beautiful for a 12-seat tasting menu. Catastrophic when 10,000 people show up hungry.
GPU · TOPs · GLSL · POPs

The staple kitchen

  • Ten thousand line cooks, each knowing exactly one simple recipe — all cooking simultaneously.
  • Simple, nutritious food: rice, bread, dal. Nothing fancy per plate — but it feeds a stadium.
  • A GLSL shader is the recipe card written once: "here's how to make one plate." Describe one pixel; feed everyone.

Neither kitchen is better. The classic pattern to avoid: asking the master chef to plate 10,000 servings of rice — or asking the line cooks to improvise a tasting menu.

The border tax

The two kitchens are in
different buildings

Carrying food between them takes time. And in one direction especially — staple kitchen → fine dining (GPU → CPU readback) — the whole fine-dining kitchen stands waiting at the door.

Module 0 just names that the door exists and has a price. Where the doors are in each family — and how to avoid pointless trips — that's the job of every module after this one.
Exercise · observation, not construction

Watch the kitchen work

  1. Open the prepared chain — you observe, you don't build.
  2. Turn on the cook-inspection view. Watch the network sit there: heat lamps on, cooking nothing.
  3. Change one parameter, once. Watch exactly one path light up — once — then go quiet.
  4. Open the twin network: same nodes, one of them time-dependent for no reason. Watch the whole chain churn every frame. Compare cook times side by side.
Same menu. Same food coming out. One kitchen is idle and one is on fire — and nothing in the network layout tells you which. Only watching the cook does.
